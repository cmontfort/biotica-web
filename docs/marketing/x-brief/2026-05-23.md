### Target 1 — @PeterAttiaMD

**Find:** Two Attia lanes not yet rotated. (a) VO2max ceiling and the
case for hard intervals on top of Zone 2, the Norwegian 4x4 lane Attia
returns to repeatedly. Pulls "isn't Zone 2 enough" replies from people
who have settled into the easy stuff because they read the easy stuff
first. (b) Lp(a), lipoprotein-little-a, the once-in-a-lifetime
genetic-risk number that does not move much with diet and is almost
never on a standard lipid panel. Pulls "I have never heard of this"
replies, which is exactly the audience the lane is for. The VO2max
lane is the higher-engagement entry because nearly everyone reading
Attia has already heard Zone 2 but has not actually programmed
intervals.

**Best fit angle for Biotica:** Both lanes are about a number whose
job is to point you at action. VO2max sits in the wearable as an
estimate that mostly drifts unless you train the top end on purpose.
Lp(a) sits behind a single blood draw that almost nobody orders. The
reply lands when it moves the reader from "interesting number" to
"what would change in my next block because of it."

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about VO2max and Zone 2 intervals:**

> Zone 2 builds the engine. The top end is what extends it. Most of
> the VO2max gain comes from the work that hurts: 4x4 minutes at near
> max, full hard, with three minutes of easy in between. Zone 2 alone
> will hold a number. It will not pull it up at the rate the
> longevity case wants.
>
> The reason most people stall on Zone 2 is that it is honest, easy
> to schedule, and the wearable estimate barely drifts. One block of
> two interval sessions a week, hard, logged against the wearable
> estimate, settles it. If the number moves, you found a missing
> piece. If it does not, you can stop wondering and put the time
> back into Zone 2 volume. Right now most people are guessing because
> they have never run the experiment.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about Lp(a):**

> Lp(a) is the lipid number that does not move much with diet, does
> not move much with exercise, and is almost never on the standard
> panel. It is a genetic-risk read you mostly get once. High Lp(a)
> changes how aggressive the other lipid levers (ApoB, blood
> pressure, all of the modifiable inputs) need to be, because the
> baseline is already pulling.
>
> The practical step is small and one-time. Ask for Lp(a) on the
> next blood draw. Then file the number with the rest of your lipid
> history so future ApoB and LDL-C reads sit next to it, not in
> isolation. A single number with no context is hard to act on. The
> same number alongside the rest of your panel and your training
> block is what turns it into a decision instead of a worry.

### Target 2 — @hubermanlab

**Find:** Two Huberman lanes not yet rotated. (a) Cold exposure
timing relative to lifting, the post-workout cold-plunge question
where the timing matters more than the modality. Pulls "but I love
cold after lifting" replies. (b) NEAT, non-exercise activity
thermogenesis, the daily-movement number outside training that
quietly explains a lot of why two people on the same calorie target
and the same program get different results. Pulls "I lift four
days a week, why is the cut not working" replies. Look for a clip or
thread from the last 48 hours touching either. The NEAT lane is the
warmer entry; almost every dieting failure has a NEAT story under it.

**Best fit angle:** Both are inputs that are already present in
someone's day and almost never measured. Cold timing is a small
behavioral switch with a real hypertrophy cost if you get it wrong
right after lifting. NEAT is the unmeasured part of the day that
the wearable is already counting. The reply lands when it points
to a measurable change the reader can run and read against their
own log.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about cold exposure and training timing:**

> Cold and lifting do not mix well in close proximity. There is solid
> work showing that an ice bath or cold plunge in the first few hours
> after a hard resistance session blunts the hypertrophy signal. The
> inflammation cold is suppressing is part of the same response that
> drives the adaptation. Cold itself is not the problem. The window
> next to the lift is.
>
> The simple fix is to put cold and lifting on different days, or
> several hours apart. Cold on rest days, after Zone 2, or in the
> morning well before an evening session, is fine. Cold ten minutes
> after the last set is not. If somebody loves the post-lift plunge
> for how it feels, that is a real value, but it is paying for the
> feeling with adaptation. Worth knowing the trade is there before
> deciding to keep it.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about NEAT:**

> The variable that quietly settles most "I am eating right and not
> losing" stories is NEAT, the calories from walking, fidgeting, and
> incidental movement outside training. Two people on the same
> program and the same calorie target can sit hundreds of calories
> a day apart on NEAT, and over a month that is the entire deficit
> or the entire surplus. Training is the small part of the day.
> Everything else is most of it.
>
> The wearable is already counting steps and rough movement minutes.
> Pull a two-week average of step count and active minutes, then
> watch what happens to it when the cut gets deeper. NEAT silently
> drops as a defense against a deficit, and that drop is exactly
> the stall most people attribute to a broken metabolism. It is
> not broken. It is being measured wrong because the floor is moving.
> The log is the only place you see it.

### Target 3 — @BioLayne (Layne Norton)

**Find:** Two Layne lanes not yet rotated. (a) Per-meal protein
distribution, the case that hitting a daily protein number is not
enough if it all lands in two meals; spreading 0.3-0.4 g/lb across
3-5 feedings is the version that drives muscle protein synthesis
across the day. Pulls "I just hit the daily total" replies from
people who eat once at dinner. (b) Reverse dieting after a cut, the
honest version (slow, structured calorie increase to find new
maintenance) versus the influencer version (an excuse to eat more
without a plan). Pulls long replies from people coming off a cut.
The reverse-dieting thread is the higher-engagement entry; nearly
every intermediate dieter has either been told to do one or watched
someone fail at one.

**Best fit angle:** Both lanes are cases where the daily total looks
right and the underlying pattern is what is actually deciding the
result. Protein distribution is the same total split a different way
with a different outcome. Reverse dieting only works when intake and
weight are tracked the whole way; without tracking, it is a regain
with a story. The reply lands when it splits the version that works
from the version that does not, and points at the log as the
difference.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about per-meal protein distribution:**

> Daily protein totals do most of the work. The pattern matters at
> the edges. Hitting 0.7 g/lb a day in two meals is not the same
> stimulus as hitting it across four. Muscle protein synthesis is
> per-meal driven once you clear the leucine threshold, and you can
> only clear that threshold so many times in a day. Two big meals
> hit it twice. Four moderate meals hit it four times.
>
> The fix is small and free. Same total, more landings. Three to
> five protein-anchored feedings, each one big enough to clear the
> threshold for your body weight. The total stays where it was.
> The signal across the day is different. For most intermediate
> lifters this is not a huge effect, but it is a free one, and over
> a year it compounds in the direction you wanted anyway.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about reverse dieting:**

> Reverse dieting has two versions and they are not the same thing.
> The honest version is a slow, tracked calorie increase off the
> end of a cut, looking for the new maintenance number that holds
> the new lower weight. Small bumps every week or two, weight
> averaged across the week, and you stop when the trend turns up.
> It is a measurement project with a result.
>
> The influencer version is "eat more, your metabolism will
> recover," with no number on intake and no number on weight. That
> one almost always lands as a regain with a flattering name. Same
> behavior, different outcome, and the only thing separating them
> is whether the log stayed on. If you can show me a weight average
> and a calorie average from a reverse, you are doing the honest
> version. If you cannot, the reverse is happening to you, not the
> other way around.

### Target 4 — @JeffNippard

**Find:** Two Nippard lanes not yet rotated. (a) Periodization,
specifically the linear vs undulating vs block question and whether
any of it survives once autoregulation enters the picture. Pulls
"do I need a periodized program" replies from intermediate lifters
running the same template for a year. (b) Split selection, the
push/pull/legs vs upper/lower vs full-body question, and how the
right answer depends on training frequency per muscle and the
lifter's available days, not on which split looks coolest on
paper. Pulls "what is the best split" replies, which is half of
fitness Twitter on a given day. The split lane is the
higher-engagement entry; the question is asked daily.

**Best fit angle:** Both are template-level decisions where the
common advice answers a different question than the lifter is
actually facing. Periodization adds structure on top of progressive
overload, but for an intermediate the gain comes mostly from running
something deliberate, not from picking the right flavor of
deliberate. Split selection is mostly a frequency-per-muscle
question dressed up as a label. The reply lands when it cuts the
label argument and points at the data each lifter already has on
how they recover from what frequency.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about periodization:**

> Periodization matters less than running anything deliberate.
> Linear, undulating, block, all of them beat "show up and lift
> what you feel like" by a wide margin. Between them, the
> differences are real but small, and most of what makes a block
> work is whether load and volume are actually planned and tracked,
> not which schedule it follows on paper.
>
> Once autoregulation is in the picture, a lot of the differences
> compress further. A lifter using RPE to set daily load is already
> reading a kind of wave inside the week. The thing periodization
> still adds at the intermediate level is a deliberate exit point:
> a block ends, you deload, you test where the numbers are. The
> structure forces a measurement. Without that, the program drifts
> into "another month of the same thing" and the log shows it.
> Pick a flavor, run it for eight to twelve weeks, test, change
> one thing. That is most of what the periodization conversation
> is actually paying for.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about split selection:**

> The split-selection question is mostly a frequency-per-muscle
> question wearing different labels. Push/pull/legs at six days a
> week hits each muscle twice. Upper/lower at four days a week
> hits each muscle twice. Full-body at three days a week, with
> sensible exercise selection, hits each muscle three times. All
> three are working for the same reason: frequency, volume, and
> recovery line up with what the lifter can actually do.
>
> The right answer is downstream of how many days you can train
> hard, how fast you recover from a given dose, and whether the
> first session of the week is still strong at the same load.
> That is in your log. A four-day upper/lower lifter whose
> Thursday sessions read flat every week is probably under-recovered
> and the split is fine. A six-day PPL lifter whose loads have
> not moved in two months is probably accumulating fatigue and
> needs the dose down, not the split changed. The split is a
> tool. The data tells you which tool fits.

### Target 5 — @greg_nuckols (Greg Nuckols)

**Find:** Two Nuckols lanes not yet rotated. (a) RIR accuracy, the
finding that most lifters consistently under-estimate how many
reps they have in the tank, especially on compounds, which means
"three RIR" in the field is often closer to five. Pulls "I am
honest about my RIR" replies, which is the exact set of people the
finding is about. (b) Effect sizes in training research, the
practice of reading past "the result was significant" to the
question of how big the effect actually was, and whether the size
of it matters next to the cost of getting it. Pulls "the study
showed X works" replies. Look for either from the last 48 hours.
The RIR lane is the warmer entry because every lifter has an
opinion about how close they go.

**Best fit angle:** Both lanes are the Biotica thesis at the level
of how a lifter reads their own data. RIR is the input most
programs are built on, and if it is wrong by two reps, the program
is delivering a different stimulus than it thinks. Effect sizes
turn "the study supports it" into "the study supports a small
effect that costs me an extra hour a week," which is a different
decision. The reply lands when it grants the principle and then
points at how the reader could check it against their own log.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about RIR accuracy:**

> The cleanest finding on RIR is that most lifters under-call it,
> especially on compounds. "Three reps in reserve" in the field is
> often actually five. Which means a program written to a three-RIR
> target is delivering a five-RIR stimulus, and the lifter is
> wondering why progress is slow on a program that looks correct on
> paper.
>
> The cheap calibration is the one most people skip. A few times a
> year, on a smaller movement, take a set to genuine failure and
> count how many reps past your "two RIR" call it took. If the
> answer is four or five, your RIR is drifting wide and the program
> is paying for it. If the answer is two, your read is honest and
> the issue is somewhere else. Either way, the log is the only
> place the question gets settled, and almost nobody runs the test.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about effect sizes:**

> Statistical significance is the easy half of reading a training
> study. Effect size is the half that decides whether the finding
> changes anything for you. A method can be reliably better than
> another method and still better by an amount that does not
> justify the cost of adopting it. Reliably and meaningfully are
> different verdicts.
>
> The honest read on most training studies is: what is the size of
> the effect, what is the spread inside it, and what would the same
> effort buy if I spent it elsewhere. A small reliable gain on a
> lever that takes one extra hour a week is a worse trade than a
> small reliable gain on a lever you were already going to do
> anyway. Population-level results give you the direction. Whether
> the size of the effect is worth your time is a question your own
> log eventually has to answer, because the cost of the change is
> paid in your hours, not the cohort's.
