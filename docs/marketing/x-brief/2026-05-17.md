### Target 1 — @PeterAttiaMD

**Find:** Two productive lanes Peter has not been rotated on in this brief
series yet: (a) VO2max protocols (the four-by-four norwegian intervals and the
"how do you actually test VO2max outside a lab" question), and (b) DEXA scans
and longitudinal lean mass tracking. Look for any thread or podcast clip from
the last 48 hours touching either. Threads where his audience is asking "how
often should I get a DEXA" or "is my Garmin VO2max estimate any good" are warm
entry points.

**Best fit angle for Biotica:** Peter's audience books expensive tests and
then has no way to keep the result alive in their training. A DEXA in March
and a DEXA in September is two data points six months apart. The lean mass
trend that lives between those two points is invisible without a connected
read. Same problem with VO2max estimates from wearables. The Biotica thesis
is that the test result is only useful if it shows up next to the training
data that produced it.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about VO2max protocols or estimated VO2max from wearables:**

> The wearable VO2max estimate is in the right neighborhood for trending, not
> for absolute targeting. The estimate moves when your training moves, which
> is the part that actually matters.
>
> The four-by-four protocol works the same way. Your fitness adapts faster
> than the number on the watch updates, and the lag is fine if you are
> reading the trend. The mistake people make is grading themselves on the
> single weekly readout instead of the slope.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about DEXA scans / longitudinal body composition:**

> The most useful thing a DEXA does is anchor a trend. The single scan is
> mostly a curiosity. Two scans six months apart with no training and
> recovery data between them is also mostly a curiosity.
>
> The version that earns its cost is a DEXA paired with weekly lean mass on a
> reasonable scale and a real training log next to it. The DEXA confirms
> what the cheaper signals were already showing. Without the cheaper signals
> running underneath, the scan tells you something happened but not why.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about cold exposure / cold plunge timing:**

> The post-lift cold plunge question is mostly an empirical one. If you cold
> plunge inside about 4 hours of a hypertrophy session the next-day soreness
> drops, which feels great, and the adaptation also takes a hit, which does
> not show up for a week or two.
>
> Plunge on rest days or in the morning and you keep the recovery feel
> without paying the hypertrophy tax. That trade only becomes legible if you
> are watching session-over-session load and lean mass on a multi-week
> window, not how your legs feel on Tuesday.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about magnesium variants for sleep:**

> Magnesium glycinate moved sleep latency for me. Threonate did not in any
> way I could see in the data. Both have evidence behind them. The personal
> experiment is the only thing that actually settles it.
>
> Two weeks on, two weeks off, looking at sleep latency and deep sleep
> minutes next to the supplement adherence log. That is the loop. Reading
> one paper and committing to a daily pill forever is the wrong shape for a
> question that has a per-person answer.

### Target 3 — @BioLayne (Layne Norton)

**Find:** Layne runs hot daily. Two angles not yet rotated in this brief
series: (a) protein distribution per meal (the 0.4 g/kg per meal threshold
question and the leucine bolus debate) and (b) TDEE estimation versus
measured intake (why every TDEE calculator is wrong by a meaningful margin
for an individual). Quote-tweet arguments under either are warmest.

**Best fit angle:** Layne's audience has heard the science and is past the
beginner questions. The leverage in a reply is to grant the evidence and add
the implementation note that turns the principle into a weekly decision.
Treats the audience as adults who are already running the protocol.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about protein per meal / leucine threshold:**

> The per-meal threshold is real but the bigger lever for most people is the
> meal count itself. Three meals at 50 g lands the day. Two meals at 75 g
> usually does not, even though the daily total is identical.
>
> The reason it slips is the second meal gets compressed when life happens
> and the day ends up at 100 g of protein instead of 150 g. That is the kind
> of pattern you only catch by looking at the week, not the day.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about TDEE estimation accuracy:**

> The TDEE number off any calculator is a starting bracket, not an answer.
> The real number falls out of two weeks of actual intake against actual
> weight change, and it usually lands 200-400 kcal off the estimate in
> either direction.
>
> The reason most people never converge on their real TDEE is they change
> their intake every few days based on the scale and never run a clean
> two-week block. Without a stable baseline you are guessing at noise.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about range of motion / lengthened partials:**

> Lengthened partials cost more in next-session recovery than the full ROM
> version of the same lift, in my data. The hypertrophy edge in the
> literature is real. The recovery cost is also real and it is not in the
> program template.
>
> Running them in the last 2-3 weeks of a block reads cleanly. Running them
> as the default rep for an entire mesocycle eats the back end of the block
> on readiness. Different tool, different use.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about eccentric tempo / time under tension:**

> Slow eccentrics get treated like a free intensification but they show up
> in the recovery data as roughly equivalent to adding a set per movement.
>
> Useful when the goal is more stimulus from the same total volume. Bad idea
> the week before a heavy testing session. The cost is real, it is just
> displaced by 24-48 hours, which is why people undercount it.
