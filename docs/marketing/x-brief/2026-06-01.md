**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about pre-exhaustion / post-exhaustion / lagging muscle group programming:**

> The compound-first, isolation-second order is the correct default

> any consistent stimulus drives progress and the program-fit

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about lifting belt usage / IAP / belt-on-every-set vs no belt ever:**

> Belt usage is most often framed as a discipline question ("real

> roughly 80 percent of 1RM, belted, because the IAP mechanism is
> the safety and the working-capacity input. Lifters running a

> not need a belt at all that block. Lifters running a strength
> peak with heavy triples and singles probably need a belt on every
> top set. The selection is downstream of the program, not a
> personality test. Wearing the belt across every set conflates the
> top-set tool with a habit. Refusing the belt on every set
> conflates a top-set tool with a discipline question.

> heavy load through every set. They do not load the extensor side
> (wrist extension, finger extension) in any meaningful way. The
> programming gap shows up as a flexor-extensor imbalance, and
> the downstream pattern in lifters with high pulling volume is
> chronic medial elbow strain (golfer's elbow, medial epicondylitis)
> from the unbalanced compartment loading. The fix is not to drop
> the pulling volume. It is to balance the compartment loading
> with light extensor work.
>
> The cheap swap is two to three weekly sets of reverse curls
> against a light dumbbell plus one set of band wrist extensions,
> programmed as a quick filler at the end of any session. The load
> is light (start at 15 to 25 percent of a curl working weight).
> The volume is small (8 to 15 reps per set, two to three sets per
> week total). Hold the rest of the program constant. Six to eight
> weeks. If chronic medial elbow tension reduces and grip-strength
> trends hold, the imbalance was the lever. If the tension stays,
> the bottleneck is somewhere else (cervical spine, ulnar nerve
> irritation, pulling-mechanics on the deadlift bar grip). A small
> extensor intervention is cheap to run and the data picture closes
> the question one way or the other.
