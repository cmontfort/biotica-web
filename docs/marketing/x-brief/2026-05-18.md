### Target 1 — @PeterAttiaMD

**Find:** Two fresh Peter lanes today: (a) continuous glucose monitors and the
"is a CGM useful for non-diabetics" question that he has staked out a clear
position on, and (b) post-meal walking and the postprandial glucose response.
Both pull threads where his audience is asking "is the data I am collecting
actually useful or am I just chasing noise." Look for any podcast clip or
thread from the last 48 hours touching either.

**Best fit angle for Biotica:** A CGM is the cleanest example of a wearable
that produces a torrent of data and almost no downstream change in behavior
for most users. The reading lives in a separate app from the training and the
nutrition and the supplements. The pattern that would actually matter (this
meal at this time of day after this training session put me at this curve)
takes a multi-stream read to surface, which nobody runs.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about CGM use in non-diabetics:**

> The CGM is the cleanest example of a wearable that prints a lot of data and
> changes almost nothing. The curve after lunch is interesting on day one and
> wallpaper by day fourteen.
>
> The version that earns the cost is reading the curve against the training
> session that preceded it and the macros that produced it. Same meal after a
> heavy leg day and after a rest day are not the same data point. Almost
> nobody runs that cross-read, which is why the device gets returned in a
> month.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about post-meal walking and postprandial glucose:**

> The 10-minute walk after a meal is one of the rare interventions where the
> acute effect is large enough to see in the data on the same afternoon.
> Postprandial spike comes down a clean 20-40 mg/dL for a moderate-carb meal.
>
> The catch is the walk has to compound across weeks to change anything that
> matters longer term. The acute curve is real. Whether you keep the habit
> when life gets compressed is the part you only learn from looking at
> adherence next to the trend, not the spike chart on a good day.

**Find:** Two evergreen Huberman lanes not yet rotated: (a) alcohol and sleep

**Best fit angle:** Both topics share the same structural gap. The protocol

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about alcohol and sleep:**

> The alcohol hit on sleep architecture is one of the larger acute signals
> you can see in your own data with no other intervention. Two drinks at
> 8pm and deep sleep drops by 30-50 percent that night for most people in my
> reading.
>
> The useful piece is that it does not require belief or willpower. Run the
> experiment once with a wearable already on your wrist, look at the chart
> the next morning, and the data settles the question for you. The reason
> most people lose the habit anyway is the data does not show up next to the
> social context that produced it.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about training time of day and cortisol:**

> The afternoon-versus-morning training question has a clean per-person
> answer that almost nobody actually runs. Two weeks at one time, two weeks
> at the other, same program, watch session load and next-day readiness.
>
> The cortisol argument cuts in both directions in the literature and the
> resolution is your own data, not the meta-analysis. The reason the
> experiment does not get run is the comparison requires holding everything
> else stable for a month, which only happens if the training log and the
> recovery data are sitting on the same axis.

### Target 3 — @BioLayne (Layne Norton)

**Find:** Two Layne lanes not yet rotated in this brief series: (a) hidden
calories and the cooking oil / liquid calories / dressing audit (Layne hits
this regularly because it is the single most common reason a stalled cut is
not actually a cut), and (b) maintenance phases and "your maintenance is not
the number it was last block." Quote-tweet debates under either get long
intermediate-lifter reply chains.

**Best fit angle:** Layne's audience knows the rules. The gap is the weekly
implementation read on whether the rule is actually being followed at the
intake-versus-output level. The reply lands when it grants the science and
adds the operational note that turns the principle into a Tuesday decision.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about hidden calories / cooking oil / dressing audit:**

> The hidden-calories audit is the single highest-leverage hour you can spend
> on a stalled cut. Three tablespoons of olive oil across the day is 360
> kcal and almost nobody logs it. Two protein shakes blended with whole
> milk instead of water is another 300.
>
> The reason it works as a fix is the deficit was usually there in spirit
> and not in the log. Running a week of strict measure-everything against
> the trend on the scale settles the question. If the scale moves on the
> stricter week, the cut was real, the tracking was off.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about maintenance drift / your maintenance has moved:**

> Maintenance is not a fixed number and the cut itself moves it. Drop 8 lb
> across a block and the new maintenance is 100-150 kcal below where it was
> when you started, even before you factor in NEAT compression.
>
> The reason people get stuck in a cut that stops working is they keep eating
> at the original maintenance number minus the original deficit. The actual
> read is a two-week stable block with no intentional deficit, looking at
> the trend on the scale and the lean mass. That tells you where the new
> maintenance is. Recalculate, then re-impose the deficit.

### Target 4 — @JeffNippard

**Find:** Two Nippard lanes not yet rotated: (a) training to failure versus
leaving reps in reserve as an intra-set decision (distinct from the broader
RIR-programming lane covered earlier in the week), and (b) volume landmarks
and the MEV / MAV / MRV framework that gets pushback from "more is better"
reply chains. Either pulls intermediate lifters trying to dial in a block.

**Best fit angle:** Jeff teaches the framework. The implementation gap is the
per-person calibration on where MEV and MRV actually sit for a given lifter,
which the program cannot prescribe. Biotica reads the downstream recovery
and performance signal, which is where the calibration converges.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about training to failure / true failure versus RIR 1:**

> The case for training to true failure on every set is mostly a vibes
> argument once you watch what it does to next-session performance. Sets
> taken to 0 RIR on compounds cost about 24 hours more recovery than the
> same set stopped at 1 RIR, in my data.
>
> Useful as a tool on the last set of an accessory at the end of a block.
> Bad default for the working sets on compounds across an entire mesocycle.
> Same total stimulus, more accumulated fatigue, worse downstream session
> quality. The cost is real, it is just displaced.

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about MEV / MAV / MRV volume landmarks:**

> The MEV-to-MRV range from the literature is a starting bracket, not your
> personal number. The published range for a muscle group might span 8 to
> 22 sets per week. Your real MRV in a specific block is one number inside
> that range and it moves with your sleep and your nutrition that week.
>
> The cleaner read is a 4-week block where you increase one set per week on
> a target muscle and watch next-session performance. The week performance
> rolls over is your MRV for the conditions of that block. Run it again next
> mesocycle, the number will be different.
