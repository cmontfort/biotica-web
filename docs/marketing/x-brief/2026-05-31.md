> Heat acclimation is one of the faster-adapting performance
> protocols and most lifters in the optimization audience have
> only run it through the "sauna for longevity" framing, which
> misses the performance pathway. The mechanism is direct. Five
> to ten days of consistent heat exposure (30 to 60 minute
> sessions, sauna or hot bath at 40 to 50C, or sealed-clothing
> overdress training in moderate ambient) drives a plasma-volume
> expansion of roughly 6 to 12 percent. The plasma-volume
> increase improves stroke volume, lowers resting and submaximal
> heart rate at the same workload, and translates to a real
> performance advantage in hot-environment events plus a
> measurable cardiovascular-efficiency improvement that holds
> across cooler environments for the next two to three weeks.
> The adaptation decays within roughly two weeks of stopping
> exposure, so the protocol is a cycle, not a fixed habit.
>
> The cheap audit is one ten-day heat-exposure block with the
> wearable's resting heart rate and HRV tracked before, during,
> and at the end. Hold training load and sleep constant. If the
> resting heart rate drops three to seven beats per minute by
> day ten and HRV trends upward at the same load, the plasma-
> volume adaptation took. If the trace does not move, the
> exposure dose was under threshold or the recovery window was
> mishandled. The protocol fits cleanly into the "I want a real
> performance read on a wearable from a short-block intervention"
> question that most lifters never run because the popular
> framing is on the longevity end and the performance end gets

**Reply scaffold — if the thread is about lactate clearance / active recovery / between-set rest:**

> Active recovery is a place the popular framing ("walk it out,
> do some light cardio") underspecifies a real physiological
> pattern. Lactate clearance rate is a trainable variable with
> concrete numbers: 10 to 20 minutes of post-effort active
> recovery at 40 to 50 percent VO2max clears blood lactate two
> to three times faster than passive rest. For interval sessions
> with a target-load preservation goal (six rounds of three-
> minute hard efforts, for instance), the clearance rate
> between rounds decides whether the round-six power output sits
> at 90 percent of round one or at 70. Passive rest between
> rounds leaves clearance on the table, the lactate buffer
> drains slower, and the working capacity on the back half of
> the session degrades.
>
> The cheap test is one head-to-head interval session. Run six
> rounds of three-minute hard efforts with two minutes of
> passive rest between in week one and the same six rounds with
> two minutes of active recovery at 40 to 50 percent VO2max
> (light cycling or jogging) between in week two. Same warm-up,
> same effort target, same time of day. Pull the average power
> or pace on rounds five and six in each session. If the active-
> recovery week holds 8 to 15 percent more power on the back
> half, the clearance pattern was the lever. If nothing changes,
> the lifter's clearance rate is already trained and the
> bottleneck is somewhere else. Either way the session answered
> a question the popular framing left ambiguous.
